PC Building Best Practices

Try to keep a clean workspace, have a dedicated area for each type of part, their cables and screws. Do not lose track of screws, it can be a real pain when you misplace a couple and can't install something that needs them.

Pre-plan all your cable management, and follow through with it. It may not matter in the short-term, but if you end up needing to do some repairs, upgrades, or anything else in your PC and the cables are a mess, you'll be wishing you had done cable management from the start. Trust me, I've been there.

Make sure to test the motherboard, CPU, and GPU before putting it in the case. You can assemble it on top of the motherboard box, just need to see it turn on then you can get back to building. Then, if it doesn't turn on after you finish the build, that way you know it's something you did wrong and not the hardware being DOA.

While you are building, leave the power supply plugged in and switched to the on position, and just touch the outside of it every so often. This helps remove any static you might have built up from moving around instead of zapping a component instead. Additionally, try to avoid building while standing on top of a carpet, or any other surface that can create lots of static.

Take your time, you don't wanna rush and miss a small connector only to spend hours figuring out why your cooler fans won't spin or why your SSD won't work, and you also don't wanna accidentally clip a capacitor or other small component while installing hardware.

If you are ever unsure about what to do next, first refer to the manuals of your case, motherboard, CPU, or CPU cooler, then, if you are still unsure, feel free to look up a build guide on YouTube, I am working on my own ITX build guide and there are many other channels with great ATX and mATX build guides.
